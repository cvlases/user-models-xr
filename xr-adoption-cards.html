Sector Overview

Extended reality is not a single technology with a single use case — it is a family of interaction paradigms being absorbed unevenly across industries. Understanding where XR is working, where it is struggling, and why tells us as much about users as it does about the technology itself.

01 — Most Mature
Entertainment
Gaming · Live Events · Immersive Media
Entertainment is XR's earliest and most developed sector — the proving ground where hardware was stress-tested, user expectations were formed, and the gap between promise and delivery became most visible. VR gaming established that full immersion is compelling in short bursts but fatiguing over time. AR gaming (Pokémon GO) demonstrated that the most successful XR experiences have been ones that fit into existing life rather than replacing it.
Key Examples
VR gaming (Meta Quest, PlayStation VR) — controlled environments, high engagement, fatigue limits
Pokémon GO — mass-market AR that succeeded by being lightweight and social
Live event AR (concerts, sports overlays) — emerging, audience-facing displays
Location-based VR arcades — premium, short-duration immersion
High adoptionConsumer-readyFatigue ceilingSocial friction
Adoption Friction

Headset form factor limits session length; social stigma around wearing devices in public keeps AR from moving beyond the phone screen.

02 — High Impact
Healthcare
Surgery · Therapy · Rehabilitation · Training
Healthcare is where XR's functional value is least contested — when the alternative to imprecise visualization is a surgical error, the cost-benefit calculation changes entirely. AR surgical overlays, VR pain management, and phobia therapy have all shown measurable clinical outcomes. This sector reveals something important: healthcare XR users are often in states of high stress, limited mobility, or altered cognition, creating design requirements entirely different from consumer contexts.
Key Examples
AR surgical guidance (Medivis, Microsoft HoloLens in OR) — anatomy overlays during procedures
VR pain management — distraction therapy reducing perceived pain during treatment
Phobia and PTSD treatment — controlled exposure therapy in clinical settings
Stroke rehabilitation — motor retraining through immersive movement feedback
Clinical evidenceHands-free criticalRegulatory frictionAccessibility imperative
Adoption Friction

Regulatory approval cycles are slow; hygiene and sterilization requirements challenge current hardware; clinician training adds implementation cost.

03 — Everyday Utility
Navigation
Wayfinding · Translation · Spatial Memory
Navigation is arguably the most intuitive XR use case — information anchored to place, visible in the moment you need it. Google Maps Live View and heads-up navigation overlays have already normalized the concept. The gap between current execution and the promise is largely a hardware problem: the ideal experience requires glanceable, lightweight, socially acceptable display — precisely the problem on-body interfaces are positioned to address.
Key Examples
Google Maps Live View — phone-based AR wayfinding, mainstream adoption
Apple Maps AR walking directions — integrated into existing devices
Real-time translation overlays (Google Translate camera mode)
Indoor navigation (airports, hospitals) — emerging institutional use
Phone-native todayGlanceability keyHardware bottleneckHands-full problem
Adoption Friction

Current implementations require holding a phone up — defeating the purpose when hands are full. The use case is proven; the form factor is not.

04 — Emerging
Contextual Guidance
Reminders · Prompts · Just-in-Time Info
Contextual guidance is XR at its most ambient — information that appears when and where it's relevant, without being requested. This includes medication reminders triggered by location or time, step-by-step instructions overlaid on physical tasks, and social context cues. It's the category most dependent on sensor intelligence and most directly relevant to on-body interaction, since the body itself is always the most contextually reliable reference point.
Key Examples
AR maintenance instructions (Boeing, automotive) — step overlaid on physical component
Smart glasses reminders (early Google Glass enterprise use)
Medication/health reminders triggered by context — time, location, biometric
Social AR (name/role overlays in professional settings) — experimental
On-body XR fitSensor-dependentPrivacy tensionAttention design critical
Adoption Friction

Requires always-on sensing, which collides with privacy expectations. Unhelpful prompts at the wrong moment are worse than no prompts at all.

05 — B2B Leader
Enterprise Training
Simulation · Onboarding · Skill Transfer
Enterprise training is where XR has found its most consistent commercial success outside of gaming. Walmart, UPS, the US military, and surgical training programs have all validated that XR simulation improves retention and reduces real-world errors. Enterprise training also surfaces the clearest hands-free use case: workers need to learn by doing, with both hands engaged, making headset-based instruction genuinely superior to printed manuals or video.
Key Examples
Walmart VR employee training — customer service scenarios at scale
Military simulation (IVAS program) — combat training and situational awareness
Medical procedure simulation — surgical skills without patient risk
Industrial AR (PTC Vuforia, Scope AR) — assembly and maintenance guidance
Proven ROIHands-free essentialControlled environmentsHigh investment threshold
Adoption Friction

High upfront cost limits access to large enterprises; content creation is expensive; most solutions are controlled-environment only, not field-deployable.

06 — Consumer Frontier
Retail & Commerce
Try-On · Visualization · Discovery
Retail XR has proliferated fastest in the form of phone-native AR — IKEA Place, Warby Parker virtual try-on, Sephora's AR makeup tools — because the value proposition is immediate and requires no new hardware. The more ambitious version — immersive XR shopping environments — has struggled, partly because the friction of entering a virtual store is high when a website works fine. The most successful retail XR is the most frictionless.
Key Examples
IKEA Place — furniture visualization in your actual room via phone AR
Warby Parker / Zenni — virtual eyeglass try-on, face-mapped
Sephora Virtual Artist — AR makeup try-on, significant adoption
Nike Fit — foot scanning for size recommendation via AR
Phone-native successReduces returnsHeadset retail unprovenBody-fit use case
Adoption Friction

Immersive retail adds friction rather than removing it; the most successful implementations are the most invisible ones.

Where the personas converge

All three users want interfaces that are visible to themselves — not hidden or ambient. All three resist the headset form factor for different reasons: aesthetics (Maya), inaccessibility (James), surveillance (Priya). On-body interaction satisfies all three simultaneously.

The key design tension

Maya and James want low-friction, smooth interaction. Priya wants legibility and explicitness. These pull against each other — smooth invisible interfaces read as surveillance to Priya. The challenge is making interaction visible to the user without being opaque to bystanders.

The strategic sector insight

Entertainment leads in adoption but has low on-body fit. The sectors with highest on-body fit — Contextual Guidance, Navigation, Healthcare — are exactly where current hardware fails. On-body XR isn't competing with what exists; it's filling the gap.
